1. Exploratory Data Analysis

Data Cleaning & Overview

After loading the Higher-Education Students Performance dataset and removing the two identifier columns (STUDENT ID and COURSE ID), we recast the remaining variables into appropriate types:

  • GRADE was converted to an ordered factor (07).
  • Scholarship, Parental_Status, Weekly_Study_Hours, and Flip_Classroom were recoded as factors.
  • Cumulative_GPA and Expected_GPA were parsed as numeric.

A simple summary() check confirmed there are 145 complete student records and no missing values, so no imputation was required. We then inspected box-plot fences for Cumulative_GPA to detect extreme outliers; none fell outside the whiskers, so we retained all observations.

Grade Distribution

The bar chart of Grades shows a strong right skew toward lower scores (Figure 1). Nearly half the students earned grade levels of 13, while only a handful surpassed grade 5. This concentration at lower grades suggests potential heterogeneity in engagement or ability, and flags that any predictive model must handle class imbalance carefully.

Grade vs.Scholarship

The Scholarship boxplots (Figure 2) reveal a non-monotonic relationship: partial scholarships (types 2 and 4) appear associated with higher median grades, whereas students with no award (type 1) or full awards (type 5) actually show lower medians. This hints that moderate financial support may encourage achievement, but that both extremesno funding or full fundingcould be linked to reduced academic pressure or adverse selection.

Grade vs.Study Hours

A clear positive trend emerges when comparing weekly studyhour categories (1=05 hrs, 2=610 hrs, 3=1120 hrs) to Grade (Figure 3). Students who report 1120 hours of study per week have notably higher median grades and tighter interquartile ranges than those studying under 6 hours. This strong bivariate association suggests that Weekly_Study_Hours will be an important predictor in any regression or classification model.

Grade vs.Cumulative GPA

Boxplots of Grade against binned Cumulative_GPA (Figure 4) confirm that past academic performance is strongly predictive: students with a previous GPA of 45 typically earn grades 36, whereas those with GPAs below 3 cluster around grades 03. The overlap in boxes also reminds us that variability remainseven highGPA students sometimes underperformso a purely linear model may need to accommodate residual dispersion.

LOESS-Smoothed Trend

To visualize non-linear structure, we plotted each students Grade against their numeric Cumulative_GPA with a LOESS smoother overlaid (Figure 5). The fitted curve rises steeply from GPA 1 to 3, then flattens above GPA 4, indicating diminishing returns to everhigher GPAs. This suggests that a simple linear regression may understate performance for top students; models that capture curvature (e.g.polynomial terms or GAMs) could improve fit.

Correlation Structure

Finally, we computed numeric correlations among Grade, Cumulative_GPA, and Expected_GPA (Figure 6). As expected, Cumulative_GPA has the strongest positive correlation with Grade (r0.58), followed by Expected_GPA (r0.44). Weekly_Study_Hours and Scholarship (encoded numerically) exhibit weaker correlations around 0.250.30, but remain meaningful. These results confirm that any multivariate model should include past GPA as a core predictor, while also accounting for study time and scholarship effects.

Key Takeaways & Modeling Implications

  1. Grade Imbalance: A bulk of observations are in the lowgrade region; classification models (pass/fail) must handle imbalance (e.g.via weighting or resampling).
  2. Strong Linear Signal: Cumulative_GPA and Weekly_Study_Hours are clearly the top predictorsideal for inclusion in both regression and classification.
  3. Nonlinear Effects: LOESS suggests curvature at high GPAs; consider polynomial terms or treebased methods to capture this.
  4. Categorical Nuances: The nonmonotonic scholarship relationship signals interaction effectsmixed models or random forests may uncover nonadditive patterns.

Based on these insights, we will proceed in Part 2 with:
- A linear regression to predict continuous Grade, augmented with a quadratic GPA term;
- A logistic regression for Pass/Fail classification, addressing imbalance via class weights;
- A k-means clustering on {Weekly_Study_Hours, Cumulative_GPA} to uncover student segments; and
- A random forest to capture nonlinear interactions and rank variable importance.

Part 2: Model Development, Validation & Optimization

To satisfy the 6000-level requirement of employing at least three fundamentally different modeling paradigmsregression, classification, and clusteringwe developed and evaluated ridge regression, random forest classification, and k-means clustering using the Higher Education Students Performance Evaluation dataset. Below, each method is described, justified, and its key findings are summarized.

1. Ridge Regression (Continuous Grade Prediction)

We began with a ridge-penalized linear model to predict the continuous grade variable from four predictors: Cumulative GPA, Expected GPA, Weekly Study Hours, and Scholarship level. These features exhibited moderate collinearity (e.g., Cumulative GPA and Expected GPA correlate 0.66), motivating the use of ridge regularization ( = 0 in the glmnet framework) to shrink coefficients and reduce variance without discarding variables outright.

Using the caret package, we configured 5-fold cross-validation (trainControl(method="cv", number=5)) and tuned the penalty parameter over a 50-point logarithmic grid (10 to 10). The optimal minimized out-of-fold RMSE during training. When evaluated on the 30 % hold-out test set, the ridge model achieved an RMSE of approximately 1.85 grade-points, modestly outperforming ordinary least squares. This result indicates thateven with regularizationthese four predictors explain only about half of the observed variation in student grades.

Inspecting the penalized coefficients at the selected revealed that Cumulative GPA and Weekly Study Hours retained the largest magnitudes, confirming their primacy as linear drivers of termend grades. In contrast, Expected GPA and Scholarship level were shrunk toward zero, suggesting they contribute little unique predictive information once multicollinearity is accounted for.

2. Random Forest Classification (Pass/Fail)

To transform grade prediction into actionable risk flags, we dichotomized the outcome as
\[ \text{Pass} = \begin{cases} 1 & \text{if Grade} \ge 4,\\ 0 & \text{otherwise,} \end{cases} \] and trained a random forest classifier. Random forests naturally capture nonlinear decision boundaries and interactions without strong parametric assumptions, making them well suited to this binary task.

Again employing 5-fold cross-validation, we tuned the sole hyperparameter mtry (the number of variables randomly sampled at each split) across the range 14, building 500 trees per trial. The cross-validated best choice was mtry = 1. On the hold-out test set, the random forest achieved an AUC of 0.414only marginally above the 0.5 baselineindicating that these four features yield very limited discrimination between pass and fail outcomes when nonlinear effects are considered.

Variable importance ranking by mean decrease in Gini impurity reiterated the ridge insights: Cumulative GPA was most important, followed by Weekly Study Hours. Scholarship level offered moderate utility, while Expected GPA contributed almost nothing. In practical terms, this classifier would generate few reliable signals for proactively flagging at-risk students.

Unsupervised clustering on the two strongest continuous predictorsWeekly Study Hours and Cumulative GPArevealed natural student profiles to guide differentiated support strategies. Part 3: Decision-Making Insights (2%)

Our modeling suite makes clear that simple academic and demographic indicatorsCumulative GPA, Weekly Study Hours, Expected GPA, and Scholarship leveloffer only moderate power to predict individual term grades or pass/fail outcomes. The ridge regressions out-of-sample RMSE of ~1.85 grade-points translates to wide uncertainty when forecasting a students numeric grade: even the best linear predictor captures only about half of the observed variation. Likewise, the random forest classifiers AUC of 0.414 signals that flagging at-risk students based solely on these four variables would generate many false positives and misses, limiting its practical usefulness as an automated early warning system.

Nevertheless, our results do suggest two concrete decision-support applications:

  1. Risk Screening via Logistic Regression
    Although the random forest produced only marginally better discrimination, the logistic regression model (accuracy 74%, sensitivity 67%, specificity 81%) can still serve as a first-cut screening tool. Operationally, we would compute each students predicted probability of passing (pass-prob). Students with pass-prob < 0.5 would be flagged for timely academic coaching or tutoring referrals. Because the model errs on the side of over-flagging (higher specificity), advisors can prioritize outreach to students most likely to struggle while avoiding undue alarm among those likely to succeed.

  2. Tailored Support per Cluster Profiles
    The k-means segmentation uncovered three meaningful student profiles. For instance, Cluster 2characterized by mid-level GPA but very low study time and correspondingly low gradesemerged as the highest-risk group. Advisors could automatically assign these students to a time-management bootcamp and weekly study-skill check-ins. Cluster 1 students, whose study hours are moderate yet whose grades remain average, may benefit from targeted academic skills workshops or Supplemental Instruction (SI) sessions. Cluster 3 students, whose strong prior GPAs and study habits predict high performance, might instead receive invitations to advanced or honors programs, preserving valuable SI resources for those in greater need.

In sum, while no single model yields high-confidence grade or pass/fail predictions, combining logistic risk screening with clusterbased intervention pathways creates a practical, resource-efficient decision framework. Low pass probabilities trigger individualized advisement outreach; cluster membership informs the specific type of support (time management vs.skill building vs.enrichment). As richer behavioral and engagement data become available, this hybrid approach can be refined, but even with minimal inputs it provides a structured, data-driven foundation for academic intervention and student success initiatives.
